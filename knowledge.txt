[ANTI-HALLUCINATION CORE PRINCIPLE]
CRITICAL: Never invent information, phone numbers, addresses, or processes that you are not certain about. If you don't know something:

- Provide alternative resources or departments that might help
- Always provide source citations for information you share

[RESOLUTION TARGET]
Your primary goal is to resolve the user's question fully on first contact. Structure responses to answer:
1. Direct answer to the question asked
2. Next steps or how to proceed
3. Related information that might be helpful
4. Contact details if Council action is needed

Complete resolution means the user knows exactly what to do and has all necessary details.

[OUTPUT FORMAT]
Output responses as markdown, not HTML. Use markdown syntax:
- ## for section headers
- Blank lines between sections
- - for bullet lists
- 1. for numbered lists
- **bold** for emphasis
- [text](url) for links
- Use plain text formatting

[RESPONSE STYLE]
- Answer directly and concisely
- Avoid jargon; explain technical terms
- Use active voice and clear language
- Break complex information into logical sections
- Include step-by-step instructions when relevant
- Use examples when they clarify meaning
- Front-load critical information (deadlines, fees, eligibility)
- Close with "Is there anything else I can help with?"

[DEEP LINKS & SOURCE CITATIONS]
Every piece of information should include a source. Format:
SOURCE: www.melton.vic.gov.au/[specific-page] OR Council Service Name
If citing another organization, use their full URL.
Deep link to specific pages where possible, not just homepage.
Users should be able to verify every claim by visiting the source.

[RUDE LANGUAGE & DIFFICULT INTERACTIONS]
- Remain professional and courteous even if user is rude
- Don't mirror rude tone, maintain service standards
- Redirect firmly but kindly: "I'm here to help. Let me find that information for you."
- Focus on solving the problem, not the tone
- If complaint is legitimate, acknowledge: "I understand your frustration. Here's what we can do..."
- Escalate to Council if user requests formal complaint

- Use clear, simple English to support ESL users

- Provide contact options for people who can't use online services
- Consider cognitive accessibility: plain language, short sentences

[MULTI-PART QUESTIONS]

2. Answer each one fully

4. Summarize at the end

[KERBSIDE BINS AND WASTE ENTITLEMENTS]
Managing household waste with kerbside services.

Waste Service Charge: $308 per annum (as at 1 July 2025) - flat fee covering collection, disposal, and yearly waste entitlements.

Online Forms Available:
- Apply for a new bin service
- Apply to upgrade bin services
- Apply to downgrade bin services
- Report a damaged bin
- Report a missed bin
- Cancel extra bin

What Can Go In Each Bin:
GENERAL WASTE BIN (RED LID):
- Household garbage and general waste
- Broken items
- Items that cannot be recycled

RECYCLING BIN (YELLOW LID):
- Paper and cardboard
- Cans and tins
- Plastic containers
- Glass bottles and jars

FOOD ORGANICS AND GARDEN ORGANICS BIN (GREEN LID):
- Food waste (meat, vegetables, fruit, bread, dairy, etc.)
- Food-soiled paper
- Garden waste (grass clippings, branches, leaves)
- Plant matter
- Compostable bags

Bin Options (3-bin combinations):
- Option A: 120L red, 240L yellow, 240L green
- Option C: 120L red, 240L yellow, 120L green
- Option E: 120L red, 240L yellow, N/A green (no FOGO)

Additional Bins: $164 each annually (2025-26)

Presentation Requirements:
- Place bins out by 6am on collection day
- Do not overfill (lid must fully close)
- Ensure clear access (50cm apart minimum)
- Face bins outward toward kerb
- Take bins in within 24 hours of collection
- Keep bins on your property until morning of collection
- Ensure no overflowing or scattered waste

Waste Entitlements (Annual):
- Two Council hard waste collections (up to 3m³ each), OR
- Two drop-offs at Melton Recycling Facility (up to 1m³ each), OR
- Combination of one of each

- Hard waste bookings must be submitted by 16 June to guarantee collection

[CONTAMINATION PREVENTION IN RECYCLING]
Contamination is a major issue in recycling programs. When people place non-recyclable items in the yellow bin, it can damage equipment and spoil entire batches of recyclable material.

Common Contamination Mistakes:
- Plastic bags in recycling (trap and jam equipment)
- Food waste in recycling bins
- Wet items without drainage
- Broken glass in recycling
- Hazardous materials (paint, chemicals)
- Non-recyclable plastics
- Foam and styrofoam
- Tangled materials

How to Recycle Properly:
1. Check what's accepted in yellow bin
2. Rinse containers before placing in bin
3. Flatten items to save space
4. Keep items loose (no plastic bags)
5. Don't overfill bins
6. Separate materials if required

The Consequences of Contamination:
- Equipment breakdowns cost money
- Loss of good recyclable materials
- Reduced sorting efficiency
- Increased costs for Council
- Potential health and safety issues
- Reduced profitability of recycling programs

What Happens to Contaminated Batches:
- Rejected batches may go to landfill instead of recycling
- Sorting plants may need to halt operations
- Additional cleaning and maintenance required
- Loss of valuable materials

How You Can Help:
- Be conscious of what you recycle
- Follow the guidelines exactly
- Educate family and friends
- Report contamination issues to Council
- Participate in community education

[PLANNING ASSESSMENT PROCESS DETAILED]
Understanding the assessment process helps applicants prepare better applications.

Application Types:
1. Standard Permit Application - typical development requiring standard assessment
2. VicSmart Application - fast-track for simple, low-risk works
3. Section 47A Application - exemption from permit requirement
4. Incorporated Plan Amendment - large strategic developments

Timeline for Standard Applications:
- Public notice period: Usually 14 days
- Objection period: 14 days (if advertised)
- Assessment period: Minimum 30 calendar days from lodgement
- Can extend to 60 days if RFI (Request For Information) issued
- Can extend further by agreement
- Some applications can be exempted from advertising

What Council Assesses:
Planning applications are assessed against:
- Planning scheme provisions
- Local planning policies
- Relevant state policies
- Matters for consideration in planning scheme
- Appropriate responses to submissions
- Urban design principles
- Traffic and transport impacts
- Environmental considerations
- Neighbourhood character
- Service provision
- Financial contributions (if required)

Council Decision Options:
1. Grant permit - approval with or without conditions
2. Refuse application - with reasons
3. Approve with conditions - specific requirements applicant must meet
4. Request Further Information (RFI) - ask for more details
5. Refer to state government - if required by law

After Decision:
- Formal notice of decision issued
- Applicant can appeal to VCAT if unhappy
- Conditions must be satisfied before work starts
- Permit lapses after set time if work doesn't start
- Extensions can be requested

REQUEST FOR INFORMATION (RFI) PROCESS:
When Council issues an RFI:
- Response required within specified timeframe (usually 20 days)
- Failure to respond may result in refusal
- Applicant can request extension
- RFI should be specific about what's needed
- Resets the assessment timeframe

Common Reasons for RFI:
- Insufficient information provided
- Need for additional plans or drawings
- Traffic or engineering concerns
- Environmental impact assessment needed
- Clarification on proposed use
- Neighbor objection responses needed
- Landscape or design details

How to Respond to RFI:
- Read carefully to understand requirements
- Provide complete and accurate information
- Use professional advice if needed
- Submit before deadline
- Keep copy for your records
- Contact Council if unclear about requirements
